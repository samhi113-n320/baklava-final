Norse Mythology

Brief History

Norse mythology is a body of myths belonging to the North Germanic people that stems from the Old Norse religion. The old Norse religion can be traced back to about 500 BC with it dying out during the second half of the Viking Age, 900 AD to 1050 AD. It was during this century and a half period that the Vikings began converting to Christianity and the Norse Mythology started to form. The original mythology was passed down in the form of poetry until the 11th-18th centuries when the Eddas (two Medieval Icelandic literary works) and other medieval texts were written. This resulted in the Norse mythology being the best-preserved version of the older common Germanic paganism.

Key Figures

Gods:

Odin

  • Portrayed as one-eyed and long-bearded
  • Ruler of the gods
  • Seeker of knowledge
  • Tried to prevent fate
  • Killed by Fenrir, which was a prophesied fate

Thor

  • Originally described with red hair, however, there are no original depictions
  • God of thunder
  • Wielded the hammer known as Mjölnir
  • Had the bad habit of overindulging in food and alcohol
  • Slayed the monstrous serpent, Jörmungandr, took nine steps, then died to the serpents's venom

Loki

  • A shape-shifting god that constantly changes his appearance
  • The god of trickery
  • Was friendly with the gods until he engineered the death of Baldr
  • Was bound to a rock by the entrails of one of his sons
  • At the start of Ragnarök, Loki and Heimdallr, guardian god of Asgard (home of the gods), slay each other

Váli

  • Born with the sole purpose of avenging his brother, Baldr
  • Grew to adult hood within one day of birth
  • Slayed Baldr's killer, Höðr who was the blind son of Odin and tricked by Loki
  • One of few to be prophesied to survive Ragnarök

Beasts:

Fenrir

  • A large wolf and son of Loki
  • Was bound by invisible bindings due to prophesies
  • Father of the two wolves Sköll and Hati Hróðvitnisson, who relentless chase the sun and moon respectively
  • Breaks free of his binds and assists in setting the world aflame and kill Odin during Ragnarök
  • Slain by one of Odin's son's Viðarr

Jörmungandr

  • The World Serpent and son of Loki
  • Was taken by Odin and tossed into the great ocean that encircles Midgard (Earth)
  • Grew so large that he was able to surround Midgard and grasp his own tail
  • Archenemy of Thor, who he later engaged in battle with, resulting in both of their deaths

Ragnarök

Ragnarök is the largest and most important event in the Norse mythology. Ragnarök was a prophesied series of impending events, including a great battle that takes place during the entirety of Ragnarök. In this battle the vast majority of mythological figures, including most of the gods, die. In addition to the great battle, there are also a series of catastrophic natural disasters, including the burning of the world, and culminate in the submersion of the world underwater. After these events, the world rises again, cleansed and fertile. The surviving gods will meet with the gods who return (most notably, Baldr and Höðr). The Earth is repopulated by the two human survivors, Líf and Lífþrasir, who survived by hiding in a wood called Hoddmímis holt.
